\section*{The Big Question: How Much Does a Transformation Skew Space?}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Every time we switch from Cartesian coordinates to polar, cylindrical, or spherical coordinates, we are applying a \emph{transformation} --- a rule that maps one coordinate system to another.  When we integrate over a transformed region, we can't just swap the symbols; we have to account for the fact that the transformation \emph{changes sizes}.  A small square in $(r,\theta)$-space does \emph{not} correspond to a same-sized square in $(x,y)$-space.

The \textbf{Jacobian determinant} is the precise answer to: \emph{how much does this transformation locally skew area (or volume)?}

We'll discover it naturally by studying the three most important coordinate systems first.

\begin{problem}
Look at the diagram above.

The small \textbf{blue rectangle} in parameter space has:
\begin{itemize}
  \item width $= \Delta r$ (a change in radius)
  \item height $= \Delta\theta$ (a change in angle)
  \item area $= \answer{\Delta r \, \Delta\theta}$
\end{itemize}

After the transformation, the image in coordinate space is a \textbf{curved wedge}.
What are its approximate side lengths?

\begin{itemize}
  \item The radial sides still have length $\approx \answer{\Delta r}$.
  \item The arc sides have length $\approx \answer{r\,\Delta\theta}$ (arc length = radius $\times$ angle).
\end{itemize}

So the area of the curved wedge is approximately:
$$\Delta A \approx \answer{r} \cdot \Delta r\,\Delta\theta$$

\begin{feedback}
The arc of a circle at radius $r$ subtending angle $\Delta\theta$ has length $r\,\Delta\theta$.  So the curved patch is approximately a rectangle of sides $\Delta r$ and $r\,\Delta\theta$, giving area $r\,\Delta r\,\Delta\theta$.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Now let's see what happens to the \emph{ratio} of areas:
$$\text{(area in coordinate space)} = \answer{r} \cdot \text{(area in parameter space)}$$

This ratio $r$ depends on \wordChoice{\choice{neither $r$ nor $\theta$}\choice{only $\theta$}\choice[correct]{the position $r$}\choice{a constant}}

So the polar transformation:
\begin{selectAll}
  \choice[correct]{Stretches area near large $r$ more than near small $r$}
  \choice{Stretches all regions by the same factor}
  \choice[correct]{Compresses thin rings near the origin to tiny areas}
  \choice{Preserves area everywhere}
\end{selectAll}

\begin{feedback}
This is why the polar area element is $dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$, not just $dr\,d\theta$!  The factor $r$ is the \emph{Jacobian} --- the local area scaling factor of the polar transformation.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\subsection*{The Polar Area Element Derived from Geometry}

\begin{problem}
Let's make this exact. As $\Delta r \to 0$ and $\Delta\theta \to 0$, the curved wedge becomes infinitesimally thin. In that limit:
$$dA = \lim_{\Delta r, \Delta\theta \to 0} r\,\Delta r\,\Delta\theta = r\,dr\,d\theta$$

Since the transformation $T:(r,\theta)\mapsto(x,y)$ maps an area element $dr\,d\theta$ to an area element $r\,dr\,d\theta$, we write:
$$dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta$$

Complete the change of variables formula for polar:
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \iint_S f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta) \cdot \answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta$$

The factor $r$ is the \textbf{Jacobian} of the polar transformation. It tells us:
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice{How fast the function $f$ changes}
  \choice{The radius of the region}
  \choice[correct]{How much the transformation locally scales area at radius $r$}
  \choice{The arc length}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
The Jacobian of polar coordinates is $J = r$.  It equals zero at the origin because the origin is a single point in $(x,y)$-space even though all angles $\theta$ map there --- the transformation collapses the entire line $r=0$ to a single point!
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
In cylindrical coordinates, the $z$-direction is \emph{unchanged} by the transformation.  So the volume element in cylindrical coordinates is built by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Taking the polar area element in the $(x,y)$-plane: $\answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta$
  \item Multiplying by the height element: $dz$
\end{enumerate}

This gives:
$$dV = dx\,dy\,dz = \answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta\,dz$$

The cylindrical Jacobian (volume scaling factor) is:
$$J_{\text{cyl}} = \answer{r}$$

Why is it the same as the polar Jacobian?
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice[correct]{Because the $z$-direction is untransformed, so only the horizontal area element gets scaled}
  \choice{It's a coincidence}
  \choice{Because cylindrical and polar are completely different}
  \choice{Because $z$ cancels out}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
Cylindrical coordinates are just polar in the horizontal plane, stacked up vertically.  The $z$-coordinate maps to itself, so its contribution to the Jacobian is $\frac{\partial z}{\partial z} = 1$ --- no stretching in the $z$-direction!
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Geometrically, the curved prism has three distinct side lengths:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Radial side: $\answer{\Delta r}$
  \item Arc side: $\answer{r\,\Delta\theta}$ (same as polar)
  \item Vertical side: $\answer{\Delta z}$
\end{itemize}

So the approximate volume is:
$$\Delta V \approx \answer{r\,\Delta r\,\Delta\theta\,\Delta z}$$

At a larger radius $r$, the curved prism is:
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice{The same size as a prism near the origin}
  \choice[correct]{Larger, because the arc $r\,\Delta\theta$ is longer}
  \choice{Smaller}
  \choice{Impossible to say}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
This is exactly why we must include the factor of $r$ when integrating in cylindrical coordinates!  Near the origin, the same angular slice $\Delta\theta$ sweeps out much less area than the same slice far from the axis.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\section*{Part III: The Spherical Transformation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection*{Unwrapping the Spherical Volume Element}

The spherical transformation is:
\[
  x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\quad
  y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\quad
  z = \rho\cos\phi.
\]

Here $\rho$ is the distance from the origin, $\phi$ is the angle \emph{from the north pole} (the $z$-axis), and $\theta$ is the usual azimuthal angle around the $z$-axis.

Let's figure out the volume element geometrically by examining what the three parameter directions do to small distances:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=2.1]

  \draw[->, thick, blue!70!black] (0,0) -- (0.0,-0.75) node[below] {$\hat{\theta}$, length $\rho\sin\phi\,d\theta$};

\begin{problem}
The key to the spherical volume element is understanding what a small step in each parameter direction does in \emph{actual distance}.

Match each parameter step to its corresponding actual length:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $d\rho$ (step outward along a radius): actual distance $= \answer{d\rho}$ (just the step itself)
  \item $d\phi$ (step in polar angle from the north pole): actual arc distance $= \answer{\rho}\,d\phi$ (radius $\times$ angle)
  \item $d\theta$ (step in azimuthal angle): actual arc distance $= \rho\sin\phi\,d\theta$
\end{itemize}

Why is the $d\theta$ arc length $\rho\sin\phi\,d\theta$ rather than $\rho\,d\theta$?

\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice{Because $\theta$ is a different kind of angle}
  \choice[correct]{Because horizontal circles at latitude $\phi$ have radius $\rho\sin\phi$, not $\rho$}
  \choice{Because of a computational error}
  \choice{Because spheres are weird}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
At angle $\phi$ from the north pole, you are at height $z = \rho\cos\phi$ and horizontal distance $r = \rho\sin\phi$ from the $z$-axis.  So the horizontal circle at that latitude has radius $\rho\sin\phi$.  An angular step $d\theta$ around that circle sweeps arc length $\rho\sin\phi\,d\theta$.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Now multiply the three side lengths of the infinitesimal box to get the volume:
$$dV = (\text{radial}) \times (\text{polar arc}) \times (\text{azimuthal arc})$$
$$= \answer{\rho^2\sin\phi}\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$$

The spherical Jacobian is:
$$J_{\text{sph}} = \answer{\rho^2\sin\phi}$$

The factor $\rho^2$ means:
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice{The sphere has radius $\rho^2$}
  \choice[correct]{Spherical shells at large $\rho$ enclose much more volume per unit $d\rho$}
  \choice{A random scaling}
  \choice{The Jacobian equals the volume}
\end{multipleChoice}

The factor $\sin\phi$ means:
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice[correct]{Near the poles ($\phi \approx 0$ or $\phi \approx \pi$), area elements are compressed because horizontal circles are small}
  \choice{Near the equator, volume vanishes}
  \choice{The Jacobian is zero at the poles for no reason}
  \choice{$\phi$ cancels out}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
Both factors perfectly capture the geometry of the sphere: the $\rho^2$ accounts for the quadratic growth of shell area with radius, and the $\sin\phi$ accounts for the shrinking horizontal circles as we approach the poles.  This is why $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Now let's see the spherical volume element in action.

\textbf{The volume of a sphere of radius $R$:}
$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^R \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$$

Evaluate the $\rho$ integral first:
$$\int_0^R \rho^2\,d\rho = \answer{R^3/3}$$

Now evaluate the $\phi$ integral:
$$\int_0^{\pi} \sin\phi\,d\phi = \Big[-\cos\phi\Big]_0^{\pi} = \answer{2}$$

Finally the $\theta$ integral:
$$\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = \answer{2\pi}$$

Putting it all together:
$$V = \frac{R^3}{3} \cdot 2 \cdot 2\pi = \answer{\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}}$$

\begin{feedback}
The spherical Jacobian $\rho^2\sin\phi$ does all the geometric heavy lifting!  Without it we'd just get $2\pi^2 R^3$ --- completely wrong.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\section*{Part IV: The Jacobian --- The General Story}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection*{From Geometry to Algebra}

We saw the Jacobian emerge geometrically for polar, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates.  Now let's see where it comes from in general.

Given \emph{any} transformation $T:(u,v)\mapsto(x,y)$:
\[
  x = x(u,v),\qquad y = y(u,v),
\]
how does a small $\Delta u \times \Delta v$ rectangle transform?

The two edges of the rectangle map approximately to:
\[
  \vec{e}_u = \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\Delta u,\; \frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\Delta u\right), \qquad
  \vec{e}_v = \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\Delta v,\; \frac{\partial y}{\partial v}\Delta v\right).
\]

The image is approximately a \emph{parallelogram} spanned by $\vec{e}_u$ and $\vec{e}_v$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.05]

%--- LEFT: small rectangle in uv-space ---
\begin{scope}[xshift=0cm]
  \draw[->](-0.2,0)--(3.3,0) node[right]{$u$};
  \draw[->](0,-0.2)--(0,2.8) node[above]{$v$};

\begin{definition}
For a transformation $T:(u,v)\mapsto(x,y)$ defined by $x = x(u,v)$, $y = y(u,v)$, the \textbf{Jacobian matrix} is:
$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix}
\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v}\\[10pt]
\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v}
\end{bmatrix}$$

The \textbf{Jacobian determinant} is:
$$J = \det(\mathbf{J}) = \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u}\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v}\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$

The absolute value $|J|$ is the \textbf{local area scaling factor}: the transformation maps a tiny area element $du\,dv$ to an area element $|J|\,du\,dv$ in the $(x,y)$-plane.
\end{definition}

\begin{problem}
The parallelogram spanned by vectors $\vec{e}_u = (a,b)$ and $\vec{e}_v = (c,d)$ has area:
$$\text{Area} = |ad - bc|$$

This is exactly the absolute value of the $2\times 2$ determinant $\begin{vmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{vmatrix}$.

The area of the image parallelogram (scaling by $\Delta u\,\Delta v$) is:
$$\Delta A \approx \left|\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\right|\Delta u\,\Delta v = \answer{|J|}\,\Delta u\,\Delta v$$

So the Jacobian determinant is:
\begin{multipleChoice}
  \choice{An arbitrary formula}
  \choice{The length of the image vectors}
  \choice[correct]{The area of the parallelogram formed by the images of the unit basis vectors}
  \choice{The sum of the partial derivatives}
\end{multipleChoice}

\begin{feedback}
This is the deep geometric meaning of the Jacobian: it is the area of the parallelogram formed when you push the two parameter-space unit vectors through the (locally linearized) transformation.  A large Jacobian means the transformation is stretching; a small Jacobian means it's compressing.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Let's verify that the polar Jacobian comes from this formula.

For $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$:

$$\frac{\partial x}{\partial r} = \answer{\cos\theta}, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} = \answer{-r\sin\theta}$$

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial r} = \answer{\sin\theta}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta} = \answer{r\cos\theta}$$

The Jacobian determinant is:
$$J = (\cos\theta)(r\cos\theta) - (-r\sin\theta)(\sin\theta) = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = \answer{r}$$

This matches the geometric derivation. $\checkmark$

The two column vectors of $\mathbf{J}$ are $\vec{e}_r = (\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$ and $\vec{e}_\theta = (-r\sin\theta, r\cos\theta)$.

Their lengths:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $|\vec{e}_r| = \answer{1}$ (unit radial vector)
  \item $|\vec{e}_\theta| = \answer{r}$ (tangent to a circle of radius $r$)
\end{itemize}

These vectors are perpendicular (polar coordinates are \emph{orthogonal}), so the parallelogram is a rectangle of area $1 \cdot r = r$ --- exactly the Jacobian! $\checkmark$

\begin{feedback}
For orthogonal coordinate systems (polar, cylindrical, spherical), the Jacobian is simply the product of the scale factors along each coordinate direction.  For polar: scale factors are $1$ (radial) and $r$ (angular), giving $J = 1 \cdot r = r$.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\textbf{(1) Scaling} ($x=2u$, $y=3v$):  The unit square maps to a $\answer{2}\times\answer{3}$ rectangle.  The Jacobian is $|J| = \answer{6}$.  This Jacobian is \wordChoice{\choice[correct]{constant everywhere}\choice{position-dependent}}.

\textbf{(2) Shear} ($x=u+v$, $y=v$):  The unit square maps to a parallelogram.  The shear \wordChoice{\choice{changes}\choice[correct]{preserves}} area, so $|J| = \answer{1}$.

\textbf{(3) Nonlinear} ($x = \tfrac{1}{2}u^2$, $y=v$):  The squares near $u=0$ become very \wordChoice{\choice[correct]{narrow}\choice{wide}} while squares at large $u$ become wide because:

These four pictures capture the main behaviors of transformations: uniform scaling, area-preserving shearing, nonlinear stretching, and polar warping.  In each case, the Jacobian determinant perfectly quantifies the local area distortion.

\begin{problem}
Now consider the \emph{complex squaring map}: $x = u^2 - v^2$, $y = 2uv$.

Compute the Jacobian:
$$\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} = \answer{2u}, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} = \answer{-2v}$$
$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial u} = \answer{2v}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} = \answer{2u}$$

$$J = (2u)(2u) - (-2v)(2v) = \answer{4u^2 + 4v^2} = 4(u^2+v^2)$$

So $|J| = 4(u^2+v^2)$.  This tells us:
\begin{selectAll}
  \choice[correct]{Area scaling grows quadratically with distance from the origin}
  \choice{Near the origin, area is stretched the most}
  \choice[correct]{Near the origin, the transformation almost collapses area to zero}
  \choice[correct]{The transformation is degenerate ($J=0$) only at the origin}
\end{selectAll}

\begin{feedback}
This is the complex squaring map $z \mapsto z^2$.  It doubles angles and squares magnitudes.  Near the origin it crushes area to zero (folds two distinct points $z$ and $-z$ onto the same image) --- which is exactly why $J=0$ there.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\section*{Part VI: Finding Transformations and Computing Jacobians}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The strategy for applying a change of variables:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Look at the boundary curves} of your region.  If they look like level curves of some simple expressions in $u,v$, set those expressions equal to constants.
  \item \textbf{Solve for $x$ and $y$} in terms of $u$ and $v$.
  \item \textbf{Compute the Jacobian} of that transformation.
  \item \textbf{Set up the transformed integral} with the new bounds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Example 1: The Tilted Parallelogram}

\begin{problem}
The region $R$ is bounded by:
$$x - y = 0,\quad x - y = 2,\quad x + y = 1,\quad x + y = 3$$

This is a parallelogram whose sides are parallel to the diagonals.  Introduce:
$$u = x - y, \qquad v = x + y$$

Then the new bounds for the region are:
$$\answer{0} \leq u \leq \answer{2}, \qquad \answer{1} \leq v \leq \answer{3}$$

\textbf{Solve for $x$ and $y$:}

Adding: $u + v = 2x$, so $x = \dfrac{u+v}{\answer{2}}$.

Subtracting: $v - u = 2y$, so $y = \dfrac{v-u}{\answer{2}}$.

\textbf{Compute the Jacobian:}
$$\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} = \answer{1/2}, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} = \answer{1/2}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} = \answer{-1/2}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} = \answer{1/2}$$

$$J = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = \answer{1/2}$$

\textbf{Area of the parallelogram $R$:}
$$\text{Area} = \int_1^3\int_0^2 \frac{1}{2}\,du\,dv = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\answer{2}\cdot\answer{2} = \answer{2}$$

\begin{feedback}
The transformation $u=x-y$, $v=x+y$ rotates the parallelogram into a nice axis-aligned rectangle.  The Jacobian $\frac{1}{2}$ says the $(u,v)$-rectangle has \emph{twice} the area of the parallelogram --- so we multiply by $\frac{1}{2}$ to correct for that.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\subsection*{Example 2: The Ellipse to a Circle}

\begin{problem}
Integrate over the ellipse $\dfrac{x^2}{9} + \dfrac{y^2}{4} \leq 1$.

\textbf{Step 1: Transform.}  Let $u = \dfrac{x}{3}$, $v = \dfrac{y}{2}$, so $u^2+v^2 \leq 1$ (unit disk).

$$x = \answer{3u}, \qquad y = \answer{2v}$$

\textbf{Step 2: Jacobian.}
$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix} \answer{3} & \answer{0} \\ \answer{0} & \answer{2} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad J = \answer{6}$$

\textbf{Step 3: Area of the ellipse.}
$$\text{Area} = \iint_{\text{ellipse}} dx\,dy = \iint_{\text{unit disk}} 6\,du\,dv = 6 \cdot \answer{\pi} = \answer{6\pi}$$

Check: $\pi ab = \pi(3)(2) = 6\pi$ \checkmark

\textbf{Step 4: Integrate $f(x,y) = x^2$ over the ellipse.}

In new coordinates, $f = (3u)^2 = \answer{9u^2}$.

Using polar in the unit disk ($u = r\cos\theta$, $v = r\sin\theta$):
$$\iint_{\text{ellipse}} x^2\,dx\,dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 9r^2\cos^2\theta \cdot 6 \cdot \answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta$$
$$= 54\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2\theta\,d\theta \cdot \int_0^1 r^3\,dr = 54 \cdot \answer{\pi} \cdot \answer{1/4} = \answer{27\pi/2}$$

\begin{feedback}
Chaining two transformations (ellipse $\to$ circle, then circle $\to$ polar) with Jacobians $6$ and $r$ respectively is very powerful.  The combined Jacobian is $6r$.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\subsection*{Example 3: Using the Inverse Jacobian}

\begin{problem}
Integrate over the region bounded by $xy = 1$, $xy = 3$, $y = x$, $y = 3x$.

Let $u = xy$ and $v = y/x$.

\textbf{Step 1: New bounds.}
$$\answer{1} \leq u \leq \answer{3}, \qquad \answer{1} \leq v \leq \answer{3}$$

\textbf{Step 2: Solve for $x$ and $y$.}

$uv = (xy)(y/x) = y^2$, so $y = \sqrt{uv}$.  Then $x = u/y = \sqrt{u/v}$.

\textbf{Step 3: Compute the inverse Jacobian} $\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)$ (easier here).

With $u = xy$ and $v = y/x$:
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \answer{y}, \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \answer{x}, \quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = \answer{-y/x^2}, \quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = \answer{1/x}$$

$$J^{-1} = \frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = y\cdot\frac{1}{x} - x\cdot\left(-\frac{y}{x^2}\right) = \frac{y}{x}+\frac{y}{x} = \answer{2v}$$

Forward Jacobian: $J = \dfrac{1}{2v}$, so $|J| = \dfrac{1}{2v}$.

\textbf{Step 4: Area of the region.}
$$\text{Area} = \int_1^3\int_1^3 \frac{1}{2v}\,du\,dv = \int_1^3 \frac{1}{2v}\,dv \cdot \int_1^3 du = \frac{\ln 3}{2} \cdot 2 = \answer{\ln 3}$$

\begin{feedback}
When the forward transformation $(u,v)\to(x,y)$ is messy to differentiate, compute the inverse Jacobian $\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)$ instead and take the reciprocal.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\subsection*{Example 4: Matching the Integrand}

\begin{problem}
Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_R e^{(x-y)/(x+y)}\,dA$ where $R$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(0,2)$.

\textbf{Step 1: Choose the transformation.}

Let $u = x - y$ and $v = x + y$.  The function becomes $e^{u/v}$.

Identify the new bounds:
\begin{itemize}
  \item On $x+y = 2$ (hypotenuse): $v = \answer{2}$
  \item On $y = 0$: $u = x = v$, so $u = \answer{v}$
  \item On $x = 0$: $u = -y = -v$, so $u = \answer{-v}$
\end{itemize}

The region becomes:
$$0 \leq v \leq 2, \qquad \answer{-v} \leq u \leq \answer{v}$$

\textbf{Step 2: Solve for $x$ and $y$.}
$$x = \frac{u+v}{2}, \qquad y = \frac{v-u}{2}$$

\textbf{Step 3: Jacobian.}  (same calculation as Example 1):
$$|J| = \answer{1/2}$$

\textbf{Step 4: Evaluate.}
$$\iint_R e^{(x-y)/(x+y)}\,dA = \int_0^2\int_{-v}^{v} e^{u/v} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\,du\,dv$$

Inner integral:
$$\int_{-v}^{v} e^{u/v}\,du = \left[\answer{v\,e^{u/v}}\right]_{-v}^{v} = v\bigl(e - e^{-1}\bigr)$$

Full integral:
$$\frac{1}{2}\int_0^2 v\bigl(e - e^{-1}\bigr)\,dv = \frac{e-e^{-1}}{2}\cdot\Bigl[\frac{v^2}{2}\Bigr]_0^2 = \frac{e-e^{-1}}{2}\cdot 2 = \answer{e - 1/e}$$

\begin{feedback}
The choice $u = x-y$, $v = x+y$ was motivated entirely by the integrand: the exponent is $(x-y)/(x+y) = u/v$ in the new coordinates.  Good transformation choices make the integrand simple \emph{and} the region simple simultaneously.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\section*{Summary: The Jacobian as a Skew-Space Detector}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{problem}
Select all TRUE statements:

\begin{selectAll}
  \choice[correct]{The Jacobian $|J|$ is the local area (or volume) scaling factor of the transformation}
  \choice[correct]{For polar coordinates, $|J| = r$ because the arc length at radius $r$ is $r\,\Delta\theta$}
  \choice[correct]{For cylindrical coordinates, $|J| = r$ because only the horizontal plane is stretched}
  \choice[correct]{For spherical coordinates, $|J| = \rho^2\sin\phi$ because of expanding shells and shrinking polar circles}
  \choice[correct]{The Jacobian is the determinant of the matrix of all first partial derivatives}
  \choice{The Jacobian is always constant}
  \choice[correct]{A Jacobian of zero indicates the transformation locally collapses dimensions}
  \choice[correct]{For inverse transformations, the Jacobians are reciprocals}
  \choice[correct]{Linear transformations have constant Jacobians}
  \choice[correct]{The Jacobian extends single-variable $u$-substitution to multiple dimensions}
\end{selectAll}

\begin{feedback}
The Jacobian is the multivariable heart of change-of-variables.  It measures how a transformation locally warps space --- stretching near large values, compressing near zero --- so that integrals in the new coordinates correctly represent the same geometric quantity as the original integral.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
Complete the key formulas:

\textbf{General change of variables (2D):}
$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v))\cdot\answer{|J|}\,du\,dv$$

\textbf{Polar:}
$$dA = \answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta$$

\textbf{Cylindrical:}
$$dV = \answer{r}\,dr\,d\theta\,dz$$

\textbf{Spherical:}
$$dV = \answer{\rho^2\sin\phi}\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$$

\textbf{General 2D Jacobian:}
$$J = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\cdot\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \answer{\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\cdot\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}}$$

\begin{feedback}
Memorize the polar, cylindrical, and spherical Jacobians.  For general transformations, always fall back to the definition: form the $2\times2$ (or $3\times3$) matrix of partial derivatives and take the determinant.
\end{feedback}
\end{problem}
